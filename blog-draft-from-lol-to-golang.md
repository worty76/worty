# From LoL Master to Golang Backend Developer — My Story

There was a time when the most important thing in my life was hitting Master rank in League of Legends. And I did it — with one champion: Aurelion Sol.

But the cost? Over 5 years of my life. From secondary school straight through college, I was grinding ranked games like it was my job. The problem was — it wasn't my job. It was just a really entertaining way to throw away my future.

---

## The Wake-Up Call

Everyone has that moment. Mine came when I looked around and realized my friends were achieving things. Getting internships. Landing jobs. Moving forward.

And me? I was still in the same place. Same rank. Same screen. Same empty feeling after every gaming session.

I graduated a year late because of it. A whole year. That's not something you just brush off — especially when you come from a background where every year counts.

---

## Falling Into Code

When I finally decided to change, I didn't have a plan. I just started learning to code. Touched JavaScript. Explored different languages. Tried frontend. Tried backend. Nothing clicked immediately.

The job market in Vietnam is brutal. Everyone's competing, and a fresh grad with a delayed graduation doesn't exactly stand out. I needed something that would make me different.

That's when I found Go.

---

## Why Go?

Honestly? Part of it was curiosity. Go felt new, fast, and different from the JavaScript-heavy ecosystem everyone else was swimming in. But the more I learned it, the more it felt right.

Go is simple. No magic. No abstraction hell. You write code, it does what you expect, and it runs fast. For someone coming from the chaos of gaming addiction, that simplicity was exactly what I needed.

I started working with microservices. Building real backend systems. And for the first time in years, I felt like I was actually building something instead of just consuming.

---

## The Indie Hacker Dream

Right now, I'm a Golang backend developer working with microservices. But I'm also something else — an indie hacker.

I build my own things. Tools, apps, whatever I can ship. Because the reality is, I come from nothing. Poor background, limited resources, and a lot of ground to make up. Every project I build is a bet on myself.

It's all about the money — and I'm not ashamed to say that. When you've been at rock bottom, financial freedom isn't a luxury. It's survival.

---

## The Gaming Problem I Still Have

Here's the honest part: I still play games. And every time I do, I feel like shit afterward.

Not because gaming is inherently bad. It taught me tactics. Planning. Instinct. How to read opponents and adapt quickly. Those skills transferred to coding more than I'd like to admit.

The problem is moderation. Gaming is fine until it becomes your escape from reality instead of a break from it. And for someone with an addictive personality like mine, that line is razor thin.

There's a simple rule I try to follow: if you're not a pro gamer earning money from it, you need to know when to quit. The moment it starts costing you real progress in life — that's the moment to walk away.

I'm still trying to fully walk away. Some days are harder than others.

---

## Where I Am Now

I'm not where I want to be. But I'm not where I was.

I went from a guy spending 12 hours a day on Aurelion Sol to someone building backend systems in Go, shipping side projects, and chasing a dream of financial independence.

The road is long. The background I come from doesn't make it easier. But every line of code I write is one step further from the person I used to be.

If you're reading this and you're stuck in the same loop I was — know that it's possible to get out. It's not easy. It's not quick. But the alternative is waking up one day with nothing to show for the years that passed.

Start building something. Anything.

That's all it takes.

---

*— Dat, writing from Đà Nẵng, Vietnam*
